## 45-Minute Call Flow

Use this path if you do not want to jump around.

1. Start with `[INTRO]`.
2. If they ask why Appian, use `[WHY-APPIAN]`.
3. If they ask why leaving, use `[WHY-LEAVE]`.
4. For fit, use `[FIT]`, then pull one proof point from `[STAR]`.
5. For technical questions, go to `[DATA]`, `[API]`, `[AI]`, or `[TECH]`.
6. End with 4 to 6 questions from `[ASK]`.

Most important sentence:

> My strongest value is translating complex business processes into buildable solutions across workflow, data, integrations, testing, and customer enablement.

## Your Goal

Make them believe this:

> Matt can become effective on Appian because he already understands the consulting motion: learn the process, model the workflow and data, define integrations, create implementation-ready direction, test against acceptance criteria, and enable the customer.

## Your One-Line Identity

> I am a customer-facing technical consultant who turns complex business processes into implementation-ready solutions.

## [INTRO] Tell Me About Yourself

Use this as the main version.

> I am currently a Senior Consultant focused on solution architecture and requirements analysis at Booz Allen, supporting a $22M VA correspondence modernization program. My work sits between business process, technical design, and implementation. I turn stakeholder needs into clear workflow, data, integration, and architecture direction for development teams.
>
> Before Booz Allen, I was a Technical Consultant at Oracle, where I worked with cloud-native Kubernetes deployments and observability. Before that, I was a Software Integration Specialist at iCIMS, where I worked on enterprise SaaS integrations using APIs, SFTP, SAML, and OAuth.
>
> The through line is that I like customer-facing technical work. I like understanding how a business process works, turning it into a buildable system, and helping stakeholders and technical teams get aligned around the solution.

Shorter version:

> I am a customer-facing technical consultant with experience in enterprise modernization, SaaS integrations, process modeling, data mapping, APIs, and implementation handoff. My current work at Booz Allen is about turning modernization goals into executable direction for product and development teams. Earlier roles at Oracle and iCIMS gave me cloud-native consulting and enterprise SaaS integration experience.

## [WHY-APPIAN] Why Appian?

> Appian interests me because the company is focused on process automation for complex enterprise and government environments. That is very close to the kind of work I have been doing: taking complicated workflows, legacy systems, data dependencies, and stakeholder needs, then turning them into something more modern and operational.
>
> I also like that Appian is not just talking about AI as a standalone tool. The message I took from the platform is that AI becomes more valuable when it is embedded into real business processes with structure, data, governance, and human accountability. That is the kind of enterprise AI adoption I find most interesting.

## [WHY-ROLE] Why This Role?

> This role is attractive because it combines consulting, technical implementation, and customer ownership. The job description is not just asking for someone to gather requirements. It is asking for someone who can work through the full project life cycle: understand the process, define the data model, build integrations, develop the platform solution, test it, and help the customer become self-sufficient.
>
> That is the direction I want to keep growing in. I want to be closer to the build and delivery side while still using my strengths in process analysis, data mapping, integrations, and stakeholder communication.

## [WHY-LEAVE] Why Are You Looking / Leaving?

> I am not looking because of anything negative. Booz Allen has given me strong experience in modernization, solution architecture, and stakeholder-facing delivery. I am grateful for that.
>
> I am looking now because I want my next step to be more product-platform and implementation focused. Appian is appealing because I could work with customers after the sale, build solutions on a real enterprise platform, and keep developing technically while still doing client-facing consulting.

If they ask "why now?":

> The timing makes sense because my current role has built the foundation: requirements, process modeling, data mapping, integration planning, and technical handoff. I am ready for a role where I can take that closer to hands-on platform implementation and customer adoption.

At Booz Allen, I work through current-state and target-state workflows, data ownership, integration options, API considerations, and development handoff. I have worked with API contracts and SaaS integrations, identity patterns like SAML and OAuth, AWS architecture considerations, SQL/Python, BPMN/UML, and observability tooling.

> Data modeling is one of the strongest bridges from my current work to Appian. In modernization work, I have to identify what data a workflow needs, where that data lives, which system owns it, and how it should move between systems.
>
> I also have SQL in my toolkit. I am comfortable reading data structures, writing queries, thinking through joins and views, and validating whether data is mapped correctly. I would expect to ramp quickly on how Appian models data through record types and relational data models.

If they ask for a project example:

> In the correspondence modernization work, different applications owned different pieces of letter data. I had to document what data was required, whether the source application should provide it upfront, and whether a shared resource layer should retrieve parts of it. That is directly relevant to designing a clean data model and integration path.

## [API] APIs / Integrations

> Integrations are one of my stronger areas. At iCIMS, I worked on enterprise SaaS integrations using APIs and SFTP. I also configured identity integrations using SAML and OAuth. At Booz Allen, I define API and integration considerations as part of modernization transition plans.
>
> For Appian, I would think about integrations by first understanding the business process and system ownership. What system is the source of truth? What data needs to move? Does Appian need to read, write, or update that data? What authentication pattern applies? How do we test failure paths and data quality?

## [AI] AI / RAG / Agentic Workflows

> My professional AI experience is strongest around AI-assisted requirements analysis and documentation rather than production AI engineering. I use AI as an accelerator for organizing notes, identifying gaps, drafting clearer language, and improving documentation velocity. I validate outputs against SME input and source material.
>
> For this Appian role, what interests me is applying AI inside a governed business process. The job description mentions RAG, agentic workflows, prompt engineering, and generative models. I would think about that in terms of use case fit, data access, retrieval quality, workflow controls, human review, and observability. The model matters, but the surrounding process and data governance matter just as much.

If they ask how you would brainstorm AI use cases:

> I would start with the process, not the model. Where are users spending time? Where is there repetitive triage, document review, summarization, classification, or decision support? Then I would look at the data sources, risks, human approval points, and how success would be measured. For RAG specifically, I would focus on whether the retrieved context is accurate, secure, and useful inside the workflow.

Excellence is the quality side: being thoughtful about requirements, data, testing, and handoff so the solution actually works for the customer.

## [ASK] Questions To Ask

Ask 4 to 6. Since this is 45 minutes, you may have more time.

1. How hands-on is this Senior Consultant expected to be in the Appian build versus leading design and client delivery?
2. What does success look like in the first 90 days?
3. How quickly do new consultants typically move from bootcamp into client project work?
4. What kinds of applications are customers asking Appian Customer Success to build most often right now?
5. How much of the current work involves AI use cases like RAG, agentic workflows, or AI-assisted process automation?
6. What technical skills tend to separate strong consultants from average ones on this team?
7. How does Appian measure whether a customer has become self-sufficient after implementation?
8. What is the team culture like around code review, pair programming, and mentoring?
9. For someone with my background, where would you expect the steepest ramp to be?
10. Based on our conversation, do you have any concerns about my fit that I can clarify?
